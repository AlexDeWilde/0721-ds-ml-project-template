We predict minutes, not just yes-or-no

The blunt question

Will it be delayed? Yes / No

Too coarse for planning — a 5-minute slip and a 3-hour meltdown look identical.

Our question

How many minutes late?

Magnitude drives every operational decision — so we predict the number itself.

Metric: RMSE

In plain terms — on average, how many minutes off are our predictions? Lower is better, and big misses are penalised most, because those are the ones that hurt operations.

03

What drives delays

Delays build through the day

Average delay by scheduled departure hour. Early-morning flights leave almost on time; by mid-afternoon the day's delays have piled up.

Some routes and seasons run late

The busy international routes and the summer travel peak (gold) carry the most delay; short domestic hops and winter months stay close to on-time.

Most flights are nearly on time

Median delay is just 14 minutes and three in four flights land within 43 — but a long tail (gold) reaches 3,451. We keep those extremes: predicting them well is exactly what RMSE rewards.

How we built a better model

Time

When it flies

Departure hour, day and month, a holiday flag — and the leg-of-day sequence.

Route

Where it flies

Distance, timezone difference, domestic vs. international, and airport congestion.

Aircraft chain

What came before

How late the same plane's previous flight was — verified leakage-safe.

All features use pre-departure information only. We tested three algorithms with time-aware cross-validation — Linear Regression, Random Forest and XGBoost — tuned the front-runners, then added a second round of features to squeeze out the last gains.

05

What it means

What we recommend

1

Protect early-leg punctuality

The prior leg's delay is the number-one driver. An on-time morning keeps the whole day's chain on time — build recovery buffers into rotations.

2

Watch the hotspots

Focus attention on afternoon departures, the summer and December peaks, and the high-delay routes — Algiers, Orly, Marseille.

3

Mind the blind spots

Keep human oversight on rare long-haul routes and wet-lease aircraft, where the model is least reliable. It's a planning aid for typical delays, not a guarantee on rare severe ones.

4

Use it for planning

~24% more accurate than the status quo — good enough to guide advance staffing, gate and passenger-comms decisions.

Data product: Delay-Alert

Pick a flight — or let ops and the travel app pull the schedule — and get a predicted delay and risk level before the plane leaves the gate, plus calmer alternatives on the same route.

It runs the operational model (108.64 min), whose top signal — the prior leg's delay — a real ops team has in real time. Planned MVP: a lightweight Streamlit app wrapping the saved model.

The next lever: weather

  • A real-weather feasibility check found adverse gusts or precipitation lift the severe-delay rate ~45% (5.1% → 7.4%) — precisely the flights that drive our error.
  • The single most promising next lever. A full build wants airport-level weather (ideally METAR visibility & ceiling) across all stations.
  • Also queued: turnaround slack, richer wet-lease handling, and grouping sparse long-haul routes.

Weather won't transform the model — propagation still dominates — but it targets the severe tail we miss today.
